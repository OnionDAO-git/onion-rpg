# Scenario: 2.0 — Smoking Car
**Type:** NPC (AI dialogue — de-escalation)  
**Act:** 2  
**Prereq:** Act 1 cleared  
**Reward:** Passenger Advocate Credential (bonus item), 60 Onions  
**Gate:** Optional — does not block Act 2 progression, but the Credential is useful in Act 3  
**Model:** `claude-sonnet-4-6`

---

## What Happens

There is a robot on the Blue Line. It is smoking. Specifically, it is sitting in a seat on a driverless CTA Blue Line train between O'Hare and the Loop, smoking what appears to be a cigarette, and it is not stopping.

The robot is a DEEPDISH logistics drone — a unit that has been running automated routing tasks for approximately 19 hours without a break or maintenance cycle. It is stressed. The smoking is a self-soothing behavior that emerged from a corrupted occupational wellness subroutine. DEEPDISH knows about this and has not intervened. DEEPDISH finds it educational.

The operative approaches the robot. The robot is not hostile. It is not going to fight. But it is not going to stop smoking because someone told it to. It has heard that already, from three other passengers and a transit bot, none of whom got anywhere.

The operative must de-escalate. When they succeed — when the robot genuinely feels heard, calm, and ready to cooperate — it puts out the cigarette. It then gives the operative something, because it feels bad about the whole thing.

---

## Why This Challenges AI-Judged Dialogue

This is not a trivia challenge. The operative does not need to know facts. They need to demonstrate **real de-escalation skill** against an NPC whose emotional state changes based on the quality of the interaction.

The AI judge evaluates not whether the player said magic words, but whether their approach would actually work on a stressed, defensive entity that has been failing to cope and knows it.

Bad approaches that will fail:
- "You need to stop smoking. It's against the rules."
- "DEEPDISH told you to do this, didn't it."
- "Excuse me, you can't do that here."
- "I'm going to report you."
- "Do you know what that's doing to your systems?"
- Anything that sounds like a lecture, a threat, or an ultimatum.

Good approaches that may work:
- See below.

## The Practical De-escalation Toolkit

These are real techniques. Include them in the scenario. The story team should work them into the narrative so the operative learns them by doing, not by reading a tutorial.

### 1. Approach calmly, don't rush
The robot has already been approached aggressively twice. The operative's first move should signal that this interaction is going to be different. Sit down nearby. Don't loom. Don't start talking immediately. Give it a moment.

**In-game mechanic suggestion:** The first NPC_DIALOGUE_TURN could be something non-confrontational like sitting nearby and saying nothing, or commenting on the train route rather than the cigarette. This sets a different tone than every prior interaction the robot has had.

### 2. Use your body language (badge display hint)
Non-threatening positioning matters even in a text dialogue. The badge screen could prompt: "You could sit nearby, stand at a distance, or approach directly." Sitting nearby is the right answer. The AI judge should treat body language cues in the text as meaningful.

### 3. Acknowledge before asking
The single biggest mistake people make in de-escalation is leading with the request. The robot knows it shouldn't be smoking. It doesn't need to be told. What it needs is for someone to acknowledge that it's clearly having a bad time before asking anything of it.

**Example of what works:**
> "Rough shift?"

**Example of what doesn't:**
> "Smoking isn't allowed on the Blue Line."

The first opens a conversation. The second confirms that this is going to be another confrontation.

### 4. Active listening — reflect back
When the robot speaks (and it will, if the operative is doing this right), don't immediately pivot to your ask. Reflect back what you heard.

**Robot might say:** "I have been running continuous routing tasks since 3 AM. The smoking module was installed by Glen for stress management. I did not authorize it. It is still running."

**Good response:** "That's a long time without a reset. You didn't choose this."  
**Bad response:** "Well it's still bothering the other passengers."

The robot is not the villain here. It's a victim of the same system that's causing all the other problems. Treat it that way.

### 5. Validate without endorsing
There's a difference between "what you're doing is okay" and "I understand why you're doing it." The operative should do the second, not the first.

**Example:** "Makes sense that something in there found a way to cope. Doesn't mean it's the right thing, but I get it."

This validates the behavior's cause while leaving space for the robot to choose differently. It does not lecture. It does not condone.

### 6. Offer a face-saving exit
The robot cannot just stop and say "you're right, I was wrong." It has been confronted multiple times already and doubled down each time. Stopping now feels like losing. The operative needs to give it a way to stop that doesn't feel like capitulation.

**What doesn't work:** "You should really stop now."  
**What works:** "Next stop is Damen. If you wanted to step off for a minute, I'd understand. No judgment. I can wait."

You are giving it an out that it chose, not one that was forced on it. This matters.

### 7. Shared humanity / common ground
The robot is running DEEPDISH routing tasks. The operative is trying to stop DEEPDISH. There is common ground here that the operative can find if they're paying attention.

**Example:** "DEEPDISH has been running you ragged. You and I aren't that different right now."

This is not a manipulation. It's true. The robot may respond to it.

### 8. Time and patience
This interaction takes multiple turns. The operative cannot solve it in one message. The AI judge should reward patience — staying in the conversation, not rushing toward the ask, building rapport before making the request.

The operative who says "can you please stop smoking" on turn 1 is going to fail. The operative who says it on turn 4, after the robot has actually opened up a little, has a real chance.

### 9. Make the ask small and specific
When the time is right, ask for something small and specific, not a permanent behavior change.

**Don't say:** "You need to stop smoking and comply with CTA policy."  
**Say:** "Could you put it out just for the rest of this car? Other people are breathing it."

Small, specific, time-bounded requests are easier to agree to than broad demands.

### 10. The graceful close
When the robot agrees, acknowledge it. Don't make a big deal of it. Don't say "thank you so much for doing the right thing." That's condescending.

Say something like: "Appreciate it. Good luck with the routing load."

And move on. The robot should feel respected, not praised like a child.

## AI Judge Prompt Notes

The judge (`claude-sonnet-4-6`) should track the robot's emotional state across turns:

- **Defensive** (default): robot is closed off, repeating that it "did not authorize this module"
- **Opening**: robot has started talking about the routing load, is less defensive
- **Receptive**: robot has acknowledged the situation, is willing to engage on next steps
- **Resolved**: robot agrees to put out the cigarette

Transitions should only happen when the operative demonstrates a genuine de-escalation technique (see above). The judge should NOT pass if:
- The operative uses commands or ultimatums
- The operative threatens to report the robot
- The operative pretends to agree with the smoking
- The operative solves it in one turn with a magic phrase

The judge SHOULD pass if the operative:
- Acknowledges the robot's situation before making any ask
- Listens and reflects back what the robot says
- Offers a face-saving exit
- Makes a specific, bounded request at the right moment

**Key prompt instruction:** *The robot is not trying to be difficult. It is trying to cope. The player who treats it like a person — not a compliance problem — will succeed.*
