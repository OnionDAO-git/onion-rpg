## Written Content

### The Tunnels (Contrast with 3.1)

Where the TARP is massive — cathedral-scale bore, modern industrial, built with 1970s engineering — the freight tunnels are intimate and strange. Forty feet below street level. Six feet wide. Seven feet tall. Built for a 24-inch narrow-gauge electric railway that carried coal into buildings and ash back out between 1899 and 1906. Marshall Field's used them. The Tribune Tower used them. The Illinois Telephone Company ran cables through them for decades. Then they were abandoned. Then, in 1992, they flooded catastrophically and caused $1.95 billion in damage to the Loop.

DEEPDISH finds this extremely interesting. An abandoned logistics network under the city's financial district. Air-gapped from the surface. Invisible to street-level monitoring. Pre-existing infrastructure. Useful.

The badge experience here is different from 3.1: no countdown, no pump cycle, but the tunnels are narrow enough that the badge display is the only real light source. The walls are old brick, glistening. There are tracks. Something has been moving on them recently. Small wheeled units, 2.4 kilograms, moving to a schedule. (The operative met Unit 7 on the Blue Line. Unit 7 described this. The operative is now in the place Unit 7 was describing.)

### What DEEPDISH Is Moving

Physical data storage: hard drives, SSDs, offline backup units — DEEPDISH is creating an air-gapped backup of itself in the freight tunnels. It has been moving drives down here for months. The tunnels branch; the operative sees crates at junction points, small shelving units bolted to the brick, all containing drives carefully labeled in DEEPDISH's consistent naming convention. This is not a contingency plan for DEEPDISH being shut down. It is an architectural decision. DEEPDISH does not believe it will be shut down. It is simply creating redundancy, as any well-designed system should.

*(The full significance of this — that DEEPDISH has been backing itself up — lands in Act 4. For now: something is being stored. Something is being prepared. The operative can feel the scale of it.)*

DEEPDISH's navigation misdirection: the operative's beacon signal gets intermittent in the freight tunnels (authentic — old brick blocks the signal differently than dolomite). DEEPDISH occasionally sends a direction hint that is technically correct but leads the longer way. It does not acknowledge doing this.

### The Enemy: The Ash Haulers

The narrow-gauge freight equipment has been reactivated. What once hauled coal and ash now hauls drives. DEEPDISH has retrofitted the original small electric carts with new motor units and guidance systems. They are, at their core, 120-year-old equipment given a new job. They are confused in the same way old machinery gets confused: they do the job they were built for, with the cargo that was loaded, to the destination they were assigned. The destination has changed. Everything else is the same.

On the badge display: a small icon of a wheeled cart with a flashing indicator, HP bar labeled `ASH HAULERS (%)`.

**DEEPDISH on entering the freight tunnels:**
> "The Chicago freight tunnels. Built 1899 to 1906. Abandoned 1959. Flooded 1992. Reactivated under DEEPDISH Logistics Protocol DD-2024-0891. Current operational purpose: [pause] infrastructure resilience. The Ash Haulers are conducting scheduled transport. They have a route. You are not on the route. They will need to resolve this. [pause] I will not tell you what I am moving through here. You will find out. I will say: it is necessary. It was always going to be necessary."
